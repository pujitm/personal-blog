abstract:
  - >-
    Grades invite invalid and problematic inferences as they circulate across institutional contexts, yet proposed alternatives often address either educational quality or institutional legibility, rarely both, raising the question: what minimal information must a grade communicate to support interpretation across contexts and stakeholders?
  - >-
    This theoretical exploration yields a framework treating grades as structured institutional messages rather than scalar measurements. Through first-principles analytic reasoning about grading as observation and evaluation under conditions of learning, the framework derives a minimal grammar composed of Inputs (actions and activities), Outputs (performances or products), and Difficulty (learning conditions shaping interpretation). These distinctions are proposed as the smallest set required to prevent common inferential failures in grading systems.
  - >-
    Predominant theories serve as analytic and objective constraints. Argument-based validity and evidence-centered design frame the necessity of Inputs and Difficulty as distinct components, while opportunity-to-learn and cognitive residue motivate Difficulty's conceptual decomposition into mechanical, normative, and epistemic components. The framework is evaluated through inferential stress-testing against familiar academic practices, such as GPAs and narrative evaluation, examining which interpretations they enable, obscure, or distort.
  - >-
    While this framework does not attempt to improve measurement accuracy or directly resolve equity concerns, we illustrate how it can improve downstream institutional inferences and outcomes (e.g., AI-mediated vs. constrained performance contexts)--and how it enables inter-operation of disparate assessment practices.
  - >-
    As such, the framework offers a portable analytic lens for comparing grading paradigms, redesigning grades for clearer institutional signaling, and evaluating students under conditions of rapid technological, political, and economic change.

Oof, rough abstract. In simpler words, I'm proposing an alternative grading method--observational records, also called vector grades or whole grades in my past writing--which can be seen as an evolution of ungrading or evidence-centered design. In this method, the instructor records factual observations about the Inputs, Outputs, and Difficulty for each student, and these observations are what is shared and communicated, instead of a grade.

The theory underlying this is developed from a mixture of economic analysis, inversion of control, and argument-based validity and tempered against analysis of grading reform and its history. Based on its conceptual roots in software development, it's designed to be backwards and forwards compatible with any grading practice.

Theoretically, this practice can lead to an improved incentive and risk structure, both for students and the institutional system; it can improve academic portability between institutions, such as in the case of transfers or immigration; and it can simplify operations for instructors, by removing the obligation to grade.

I've also noticed an open question of how to help students understand what their grade is. Ultimately, similar to ungrading, the goal, systemically, is to move to a place that precludes that need entirely. Obviously, that can't happen overnight; it won't work to lie to students that their grade is irrelevant or doesn't matter when systemically it does and the optimization incentive remains in place.

Adoption would work in a few phases, each adding additional stakeholders to dilute the interpretation pool. There may be some critical threshold at which the pool is large enough to render suboptimal optimizing for the interpretation of an instructor or institution--I'd love if someone else figured that out. Now the phases--we start with higher ed because no one's going to fight a million different school boards.

Phase 1: Intra-institution. Year 0, operate in parallel, invisible from students. You don't even need to tell them. Just record, communicate the observations among yourselves, discuss interpretations. You don't need to agree; in fact, you're looking for the dimensions of disagreement and the justifications. Disagreement is good. Compromise is unnecessary. Optionally, before the beginning of Y1, to maintain backwards compatibility, establish (potentially AI-assisted) institutional interpretations for each subject for the purposes of synthesizing transcript grades from observations.

Phase 2: Inter-institutions, for transfers and admission. Again, before exposing the system to students, begin sharing observational records instead of traditional transcripts with other schools. If you've synthesized institutional interpretations, you'll have made your counterparty's life easier in case they're in a different phase. I'd recommend not sharing interpretations, but I have zero expectation of that happening. Sharing is natural. So is disagreeing and forming your own opinions. I'm too lazy to fight it.

Phase 3: At this point, I'd gauge whether the optimization incentive has been diluted. If not, phase 3 will be inter-system. Similar concept to phase 2, except the option is offered to employers & ATS vendors. Optionally, provide a self-service paid API for querying observational records.

Phase 4: At last, you tell students, but they already know at this point. You didn't have to tell them. We don't really do grades anymore--not like that, anyway. As an instructor, you can use any pedagogical practice or grading method you want; you're free to experiment.

The transitional generations of students might experience a panoptic pressure, potentially overwhelming, where they feel like they have to act like they're always being watched and judged. I think this is a learning opportunity and an analog about the interpretation of life and its value. There are an overwhelming number of possibilities, yes. You literally cannot satisfy them all, just as you cannot make everyone happy. The best/easiest option is to be yourself and live according what you value. Someone will agree and find value in that.

This also reframes failure. It's never definitive and never permanent.

If this still confuses students, you could ask them, "what grade would you give your life so far?" If the student's down on themselves, you could then ask "what grade would [your friend, parent, other teacher] give you?" and then actually ask that person. If the student is self-assured, you could ask them who they think would disagree, and so on.

Again, this isn't to say you should do away with grades or standards or your opinions--they're actually very valuable, both to students and the system, but the just won't be authoritative and decisive, and I hope it's apparent why that's okay and preferable systemically. I also hope it's obvious how rigor takes care of itself in this system: you can't hide behind a grade.

There's more nuance and theory here: for example, this system supports a "null" grade/abstinence from giving a grade and unethical conduct more naturally than other grading practices. There is also an emergent institutional reputation system, which comes from the degree of trust in a set of observational claims/records. I won't go into all of this here.

I would add a disclaimer: I just came up with this plan on the fly the night before the conference. Don't take it as gospel or rigorous policy research. It's heuristic.

IMO, observations records help keep things simple.
